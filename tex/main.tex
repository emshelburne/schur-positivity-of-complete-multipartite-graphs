\section{Introduction}

In 1995, Richard Stanley extended the study of graph colorings to the realm of symmetric functions by introducing the chromatic symmetric function \cite{stan_95}. This innovation sparked extensive research into chromatic symmetric functions and related power series, uncovering deep connections to representation theory and algebraic structures. Symmetric functions that expand nonnegatively in the elementary basis or Schur basis often possess significant algebraic or combinatorial properties, including meaningful interpretations of their coefficients. For instance, symmetric functions with nonnegative Schur expansions correspond to the Frobenius image of representations of the symmetric group \cite{sym_group}. A central question posed in \cite{stan_95} seeks to classify graphs that are Schur-positive or e-positive, leading to three major conjectures that continue to shape the field.


\begin{enumerate}

\item (The Stanley-Stembridge Conjecture, \cite{stan_93}): All claw-free incomparability graphs are e-positive.

\item (The Nonisomorphic Tree Conjecture, \cite{stan_95}): No two nonisomorphic trees have the same chromatic symmetric function.

\item (The Claw-free Conjecture, \cite{stan_98}): All claw-free graphs are Schur-positive.

\end{enumerate}


A proof of the Stanley-Stembridge conjecture was announced in \cite{hikita24}. Additionally, various subfamilies of claw-free incomparability graphs have been shown to satisfy this conjecture in \cite{GebSag01}, \cite{Dahl18}, \cite{centered_at_vertex}, among others. The Nonisomorphic Tree Conjecture has been computationally verified for trees with up to 29 vertices in \cite{trees29}, and further progress on this conjecture is detailed in \cite{mar_mor}, \cite{OrSc14}, and \cite{foley22}, for instance. Vesselin Gasharov advanced the Claw-free Conjecture by demonstrating that all claw-free incomparability graphs are Schur-positive in \cite{gash_96}. David Wang and Monica Wang provided a combinatorial formula for determining the Schur coefficients of chromatic symmetric functions, which facilitated various results, including a classification of the Schur-positivity of complete bipartite and tripartite graphs \cite{WW2020}. The authors derived an alternate version of the formula from \cite{WW2020} in terms of a new combinatorial object corresponding to a graph $G$, named special rim hook $G$-tabloids \cite{gen_nets}. The authors then used this formula to prove that all generalized net graphs are Schur-positive.


We employ the aforementioned formula, as well as other combinatorial arguments, to extend the result from \cite{WW2020} and determine a Schur-positivity classification for all complete multipartite graphs. First, we exploit the property of niceness, a necessary feature of any Schur-positive graph, as shown in \cite{stan_98}. We then build on the arguments from \cite{WW2020} to show that certain types of partitions, which are called balanced, always correspond to graphs which are not nice and therefore not Schur-positive. We address the remaining cases by explicitly formulating the coefficients of the chromatic symmetric functions of certain complete multipartite graphs. In order to do this, we also show these coefficients can be computed in a simpler way in the case where the underlying graph is a complete multipartite graph.


Section \ref{sect:prelims} covers the required preliminaries. Section \ref{sect:srh_G_tabs} introduces the key combinatorial object, that is, the special rim hook $G$-tabloid. Lastly, Section \ref{sect:comp_multi_graphs} includes several results which together provide a classification for the Schur-posivity of all complete multipartite graphs.

 A \textit{partition} of $n\ge 0$ is a sequence of weakly decreasing positive integers $\lambda=(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_k)$ whose sum is $n$.
 The \textit{length} of $\lambda$ is given by $\ell(\lambda)=k$ and the \textit{size} of $\lambda$ is given by $|\lambda|=n$. In the case where $n=0$, we say $\lambda$ is the \textit{empty partition}. We use exponents to indicate repeated integers in a partition. For instance, $\lambda=(5,5,3,3,1)=(5^2,3^2,1)$. Throughout this paper, we will consider the \textit{dominance partial order} on partitions. For partitions $\lambda=(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_k)$ and $\mu=(\mu_1,\dots,\mu_j)$, we say $\mu$ is \textit{dominated} by $\lambda$ and write
\[
\lambda \ge \mu \qquad\text{if and only if } \qquad\lambda_1+\cdots+\lambda_i \ge \mu_1+\cdots+\mu_i
\]
for all $i\ge 1$. For this definition, we take $\lambda_i=0$ for $i>k$ and $\mu_i=0$ for $i>j$.

Given some partition $\lambda$, the \textit{Schur function} associated to $\lambda$ is
\[
s_{\lambda}=\sum_{Q}\text{wt}(Q)
\]
where the sum spans over all semistandard Young tableaux $Q$ of shape $\lambda$. 


Next, we introduce the classical elementary basis for Sym$(\mathbf{x})$. We define the \textit{$i$th elementary symmetric function} to be
    \[
    e_i=s_{(1^i)}
    \]
    and the \textit{elementary function} associated to a partition $\lambda$ to be
    \[
    e_{\lambda}=e_{\lambda_1}\cdots e_{\lambda_{k}}.
    \]




We have that
\[
\mathbf{s}=\{s_{\lambda}\,\mid\, \lambda\text{ is a partition}\}\andd \mathbf{e}=\{e_{\lambda}\,\mid\, \lambda\text{ is a partition}\}
\]
are each bases for $\text{Sym}(\mathbf{x})$. If $\textbf{b}$ is a basis for $\text{Sym}(\mathbf{x})$ which is indexed by partitions, we use the notation
\[
[b_{\lambda}]f(\mathbf{x})= \text{the coefficient of $b_{\lambda}$ in the expansion of $f(\mathbf{x})$ in the basis $\mathbf{b}$}. 
\]
Given some $f(\mathbf{x})\in \text{Sym}(\mathbf{x})$, we say $f(\mathbf{x})$ is \textit{Schur-positive} if $[s_{\lambda}]f(\mathbf{x})\ge 0$ for all partitions $\lambda$. We say $f(\mathbf{x})$ is \textit{$\mathbf{e}$-positive} if $[e_{\lambda}]f(\mathbf{x})\ge 0$ for all partitions $\lambda$. One important fact regarding these two bases is that the elementary symmetric functions themselves are Schur-positive \cite{ERemmel}. Accordingly, the $\mathbf{e}$-positivity of $f(\mathbf{x})$ implies the Schur-positivity of $f(\mathbf{x})$. Likewise, if $f(\mathbf{x})$ is not Schur-positive, $f(\mathbf{x})$ is also not $\mathbf{e}$-positive.

Next, we define a family of functions belonging to Sym$(\mathbf{x})$ known as chromatic symmetric functions, which were introduced in \cite{stan_95}. Given a graph $G$ with vertices $V(G)$, a \textit{proper coloring} of $G$ in $q$ colors is a map
\[
\mathcal{C}:V(G)\to\{1,2,3,\dots, q\}
\]
such that, if $u$ and $v$ are adjacent, then $\mathcal{C}(u)\ne \mathcal{C}(v)$. For a proper coloring $\mathcal{C}$ of $G$, we define
    \[
\mathbf{x}^{\mathcal{C}}=x_{\mathcal{C}(v_1)}x_{\mathcal{C}(v_2)}\cdots x_{\mathcal{C}(v_n)}.
    \]
    The \textit{chromatic symmetric function} of a graph $G$ is the formal power series
\[
X_G(\mathbf{x})=\sum_{\mathcal{C}}\mathbf{x}^{\mathcal{C}},
\]
where the sum ranges over all proper colorings $\mathcal{C}$ of $G$.
    The chromatic symmetric function is symmetric because permuting the variables of the function is equivalent to permuting the colors in each proper coloring. We say that a graph $G$ is \textit{Schur-positive} (resp. \textit{$\mathbf{e}$-positive}) if $X_G(\mathbf{x})$ is Schur-positive (resp. $\mathbf{e}$-positive). 



There are certain structural properties associated with graphs which are either Schur-positive or $\mathbf{e}$-positive. For example, A graph $G$ is \textit{nice} if whenever $G$ has a stable partition of type $\lambda$, $\mu\le \lambda$ implies $G$ also has a stable partition of type $\mu$. The contrapositive and the following proposition is a useful tool for disproving the Schur-positivity of certain graphs.

\begin{proposition}[\cite{stan_98}]
\label{prop:schur_pos_means_nice}
If $G$ is Schur-positive, $G$ is nice.
\end{proposition}

 In order to discuss an important result from \cite{WW2020}, we now introduce a combinatorial object known as a special rim hook tabloid. 
 
 
 A \textit{rim hook} of \textit{length} $k$ is a sequence of $k$ connected cells in a diagram, each of which lie on the southeast boundary, and whose removal results in a smaller diagram. For any rim hook, we call each path between consecutive cells a \textit{step}. If the cells are in different rows, we use the term \textit{north step} ($N$-step), whereas if the cells are in different columns, we use the term $\textit{east step}$ (E-step). 
 
 
 
 Let $\kappa=[\kappa_1,\dots,\kappa_{j}]$ be a composition and $\lambda=(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_k)$ be a partition. A \textit{rim hook tabloid} of shape $\lambda$ and content $\kappa$ is a filling of the cells of the diagram of $\lambda$ with $j$ sequences of connected cells $r_i$ such that $r_1$ is a rim hook of length $\kappa_1$ and, for all $1\le i \le j-1$, if $r_1,\dots, r_i$ are removed from $\lambda$ to form $\tilde{\lambda}$, $r_{i+1}$ is a rim hook of $\tilde{\lambda}$ of length $\kappa_{i+1}$.  A \textit{special rim hook tabloid} (SRH tabloid) is a rim hook tabloid such that every rim hook intersects the first column. We define the \textit{sign} of an SRH tabloid to be
 \[
 \mathrm{sgn}(T)=(-1)^{\text{\# north steps in diagram}}.
 \]
 Moreover, we use the notation $\mathcal{T}_{\lambda}$ to denote the set of all special rim hook tabloids of shape $\lambda$. Given an SRH tabloid $T$, we denote by $\kappa_T$ the \textit{content} of $T$, which is the composition given by the rim hook lengths read from the bottom to the top of the diagram.

\section{Special Rim Hook $G$-Tabloids}
\label{sect:srh_G_tabs}

  In this section, we define an SRH $G$-tabloid, which is a modified version of an SRH tabloid that corresponds to a graph $G$. We then state a formula for all Schur coefficients of chromatic symmetric functions in terms of signed sums of these combinatorial objects, which the authors proved in \cite{gen_nets}.
 
 
 \begin{definition}
 \label{def:SRH_G_tabs}
 Consider a graph $G$ and a partial order $\le$ on the vertices of $G$ satisfying: if vertices $u$ and $v$ are nonadjacent, then $u$ and $v$ are comparable.
 
 
 We can then define a \textit{special rim hook $G$-tabloid} (or  \textit{SRH $G$-tabloid}) to be an SRH tabloid such that every cell is filled with a vertex of $G$ and the following conditions are met.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cells spanned by the same rim hook contain vertices which form a stable set.
    \item For each rim hook, reading the corresponding vertices from southwest to northeast order results in an increasing sequence with respect to the partial order.
\end{itemize}
The \textit{sign} and \textit{shape} of an SRH $G$-tabloid are respectively the sign and shape of the underlying SRH tabloid.
We denote the set of all SRH $G$-tabloids of shape $\lambda$ by $\mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G}$.
 \end{definition}

 % While working with SRH $G$-tabloids, it is often helpful to distinguish between different parts of the underlying diagram. 

\begin{definition}
\label{def:tail_head}
Let $T$ be an SRH $G$-tabloid. We define the \textit{tail} of $T$, denoted \text{tl}$(T)$, to be the part of $T$ containing all rows of length $1$. Likewise, we define the \textit{head} of $T$, denoted $\text{hd}(T)$ to be the part of $T$ containing all rows of length strictly greater than $1$. Either tl$(T)$ or hd$(T)$ could be empty, depending on the shape of $T$. 
\end{definition}

Let $T$ and $T'$ be SRH $G$-tabloids. We say that
\[
\text{tl}(T)=\text{tl}(T') \orr \text{hd}(T)=\text{hd}(T')
\]
if and only if the tails, or respectively the heads, of $T$ and $T'$ are equal as SRH $G'$-tabloids for some subgraph $G'$ of $G$.



 The choice of a partial order introduces flexibility when working with SRH $G$-tabloids. When $G$ is the incomparability graph of a poset $P$, a natural approach is to adopt the partial order defined by the poset itself. In an incomparability graph, two vertices are non-adjacent precisely when they are comparable. This ensures that the necessary condition is automatically met. Otherwise, if $G$ is not an incomparability graph, the simplest approach is to choose a total order on $G$ by labeling the vertices numerically.

By considering SRH $G$-tabloids, we may obtain an alternative combinatorial interpretation of Schur coefficients, as is done in \cite{gen_nets}.

\begin{proposition}[\cite{gen_nets}]
\label{cor:better_s_form}
Consider any graph $G$, a partition $\lambda$ of $|V(G)|$, and a partial order on the vertices of $G$ such that nonadjacent vertices are comparable. We have
\[
[s_{\lambda}]X_G=\sum_{T\in \mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G}}\mathrm{sgn}(T).
\]
\end{proposition}


\begin{remark}
When $G=\text{inc}(P)$, there is a sign-preserving bijection between SRH $G$-tabloids of shape $\lambda$ and $P$-arrays of shape $\pi(\lambda)$, which appear in \cite{gash_96}. Thus, Proposition \ref{cor:better_s_form} can also arise as a corollary to the proof of Theorem 3 in \cite{gash_96}.
\end{remark}

All complete multipartite graphs are incomparability graphs. In \cite{WW2020}, a Schur-positivity classification is offered for complete bipartite and complete tripartite graphs. Specifically, Example 3.4 and Theorem 3.7 in \cite{WW2020} determine that the only Schur-positive complete bipartite graphs are

\[
K_{\lambda}\qquad \text{for}\qquad\lambda\in\{(1,1,1), (2,1,1), (2,2,1), (2,2,2), (3,2,2)\}.
\]

We next prove Theorem \ref{thm:inv_paths_tail}, which significantly reduces the number of SRH G-tabloids which must be counted to compute Schur coefficients of the chromatic symmetric function of an incomparability graph of $G$.

\begin{definition}
Let $T$ be an SRH $G$-tabloid. We define the \textit{tail sequence of $T$}, denoted $\text{ts}(T)$, to be the sequence of vertices of $G$ in the rows of length $1$ of the tabloid $T$, read from bottom to top.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
Let $(P,\le)$ be a finite poset and $G=\text{inc}(P)$. A \textit{non-increasing sequence in $G$} is a sequence $(v_1,\dots,v_k)$ of vertices of $G$ such that $v_i \nleq v_{i+1}$ for $1\le i \le k-1$. A \textit{spanning non-increasing sequence of $G$} is a non-increasing sequence in $G$ which includes every vertex of $G$ exactly once. Let $N_{\mathrm{sp}}(G)$ denote the number of spanning non-increasing sequences of $G$. We note that if $G$ has no vertices, then $N_{\mathrm{sp}}(G)=1$, representing the empty non-increasing sequence in $G$. 
\end{definition}

To illustrate the prior definitions, we note that the tail sequences of the SRH $G$-tabloids in Example \ref{ex:SRH_G_tab} are respectively
\[
(e,b,f,d),\qquad (e,a,c,d),\qquad (c,f,d,a),\andd (d,c,b,f).
\]
Among these, only $(c,f,d,a)$ is a non-increasing sequence in the underlying incomparability graph. An example of a spanning non-increasing sequence of this incomparability graph (originally defined in Examples \ref{ex:hasse} and \ref{ex:inc_graph}) is the sequence $(f,e,b,c,a,d)$.

To make way for the following theorem, we now set the notation
\[
\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_{\lambda,G}=\{ T\in \mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G} \text{ such that } \text{ts}(T)\text{ is a non-increasing sequence in $G$}\},
\]
which may be used when $G$ is an incomparability graph.
\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:inv_paths_tail}
For any incomparability graph $G$, we have
\[
[s_{\lambda}]X_G=\sum_{T\in\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_{\lambda,G}}\mathrm{sgn}(T).
\]
\begin{proof}
Consider any incomparability graph $G$ and partition $\lambda$. From Corollary \ref{cor:better_s_form}, we have
\[
[s_{\lambda}]X_G=\sum_{T\in\mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G}}\mathrm{sgn}(T).
\]
Suppose $\lambda$ has $k$ rows of length $1$. We denote by $S_k(G)$ the set of all sequences of distinct vertices of $G$ of length $k$. Moreover, we set
\[
\mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G,s}=\{T\in\mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G}\text{ such that }\text{ts}(T)=s\}.
\]
Hence, we have
\[
[s_{\lambda}]X_G=\sum_{s\in S_k(G)}\sum_{T\in\mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G,s}}\mathrm{sgn}(T).
\]
For any $s\in S_k(G)$ and $T\in \mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G,s}$, we set $j$ to be the minimal index $1\le j \le k-1$ such that $v_j\le v_{j+1}$ (if it exists). We let $e_j$ denote an $N$-step from $v_j$ to $v_{j+1}$.

Now, we define the map
\begin{align*}
\psi: \mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G,s} &\to \mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G,s}\\
T &\mapsto T\Delta e_j,
\end{align*}
where $T\Delta e_j$ is the SRH $G$-tabloid obtained by removing $e_j$ if $e_j$ is already included in $T$ and adding $e_j$ otherwise.


If $v_{j+1}$ is part of a hook, it must be of the form $v_{j+1}\le v_{j+2}\cdots\le v_{j+h}$ (read bottom to top). Thus, by transitivity of the underlying poset, $v_{j}\le v_{j+1}\le v_{j+2}\cdots\le v_{j+h}$ so $v_j$ may be added to the hook. Note that there are no $N$-steps below $v_j$ since that would contradict the minimality of $j$. 


Since the sign of an SRH $G$-tabloid is given by the parity of the $N$-steps, we have that $\psi$ is a sign-reversing involution for any set $\mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G,s}$ on which it is defined. Therefore, whenever there exists a minimal $j$ such that $v_j\le v_{j+1}$ in the sequence $s$, we have
\[
\sum_{T\in\mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G,s}}\mathrm{sgn}(T)=0.
\]
We are thus left with the sums ranging over $\mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G,s}$ for which there is no index $j$ such that $v_j\le v_{j+1}$ in the sequence $s$. These sequences are precisely the non-increasing sequences of length $k$ in $G$. We conclude the desired formula holds. 
\end{proof}
\end{theorem}

Now we can immediately apply the last proposition to derive a formula for the Schur coefficients of the chromatic symmetric functions of complete multipartite graphs $K_{(2^{\beta})}$, $\beta\ge 1$. As noted earlier, these graphs are incomparability graphs.


We note that the following formula covers all Schur coefficients for the graphs $K_{(2^{\beta})}$. Since the independence number of $K_{(2^{\beta})}$ is $2$, it is impossible for a SRH $K_{(2^{\beta})}$-tabloid to have a hook of length 3 or greater by Lemma \ref{lemma:independence_lemma} and therefore impossible to have a row of length 3 or greater.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:form_two_partite_case}
We have that:
\[
[s_{(2^C,1^D)}]X_{K_{(2^{\beta})}}=\frac{\beta !}{(\beta-C)!}\cdot N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C})})
\]
for $C,D\ge 0$ such that $2C+D=2\beta$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Set $G=K_{(2^{\beta})}$. Note that we must have $2C+D=2\beta$ in order for the number of cells in the tabloids to match the number of vertices in $G$.


For any $T\in \mathcal{T}_{(2^{\beta}),G}'$, the diagram is divided into the head, consisting of E-steps and the lower half consisting of the tail. We note that since the independence number of $G$ is $2$, by Lemma \ref{lemma:independence_lemma}, there are no hooks of length $3$ in the diagram. In particular, there cannot be a $N$-step up from the top of the tail to the bottom most row of length $2$.


To fill the head of the diagram, we must select $C$ stable $2$-sets from $G$ and order them. Since $G$ is made up of $\beta$ stable $2$-sets to choose from, we have $\frac{\beta!}{(\beta-C)!}$ choices for this part of the diagram. 


To fill the tail with vertices, we must choose a non-increasing sequence in $G$ through the remaining vertices of $G$. The vertices which remain form the subgraph $K_{(2^{\beta-C})}$. Hence, there are exactly $N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C})}$ choices for the tail.


Finally, we note that all SRH $G$-tabloids we have counted are positive as there are no $N$-steps in the top of the diagram nor the tail since the vertices in the tail form a non-increasing sequence in $G$. Thus, the desired formula holds.
\end{proof}


Now that we have determined a formula for the Schur coefficients of the chromatic symmetric function of $K_{2^{\beta}}$, $\beta\ge 1$, we can employ this formula to complete the classification of complete multipartite graphs in terms of Schur-positivity.


\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:K32_s_pos}
 We have that $K_{(3,2^{\beta})}$ is Schur-positive for $\beta\ge 1$. Moreover, we have:
\[
[s_{(3,2^C,1^D)}]X_{K_{(3,2^{\beta})}}=[s_{(2^C,1^D)}]X_{K_{(2^{\beta})}}=\frac{\beta !}{(\beta-C)!}\cdot N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C})})
\]
for $C,D\ge 0$ such that $2C+D=2\beta$. We also have:
\begin{align*}
[s_{(2^C,1^D)}]X_{K_{(3,2^{\beta})}}&=\frac{\beta !}{(\beta - C+1)!}\cdot \big((\beta-C+1)N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(3,2^{\beta-C})})-N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1})})\\
&+(C+2)N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1},1)})\big)
\end{align*}
for $C\ge 1,D\ge 1$ such that $2C+D=2\beta+3$. We note that if $\beta<C$, $N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(3,2^{\beta-C})})$ is set to equal zero since the graph $K_{(3,2^{\beta-C})}$ does not exist. Lastly, we have:
\[
[s_{1^{2\beta+3}}]X_{K_{(3,2^{\beta})}}=N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(3,2^{\beta})}).
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We first note that since the graphs $K_{(3,2^{\beta})}$, $\beta\ge 1$, are incomparability graphs, Proposition \ref{prop:inv_paths_tail} applies. Fix $G=K_{(3,2^{\beta})}$, $\beta\ge 1$. We divide the coefficients into several cases. Denote the three vertices in the unique stable $3$-set by $v_1\le v_2\le v_3$.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Assume $\lambda=(3,2^C,1^D)$. Note that we must have $2C+D=2\beta$ in order for the number of cells in the tabloids to match the number of vertices in $G$.
    
    
    For any $T\in \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_{\lambda,G}$, the row of length $3$ must be filled by the unique stable $3$-set in $G$. The remainder of the diagram must then be filled with the rest of the vertices which form a subgraph $K_{(2^{\beta})}$. Accordingly, these tabloids are in sign-preserving bijection with the tabloids in $\mathcal{T}_{(2^{C},1^D),K_{2^{\beta}}}'$. Hence the formula follows from Proposition \ref{prop:form_two_partite_case}.
    % \item Assume $\lambda=(3,1^{\beta})$. The top row must be filled with an $EE$-hook consisting of $v_1,v_2,v_3$. It remains to choose a non-increasing sequence in $G$ through the rest of the vertices, yielding the desired formula. %don't need this case
    \item Assume $\lambda=(2^C,1^D)$ for $C\ge 1$, $D\ge 1$. Note that we must have $2C+D=2\beta+3$ in order for the number of cells in the tabloids to match the number of vertices in $G$. We also observe that $D\ge 1$ must hold in order for there to be an odd number of cells in the diagram to match the odd number of vertices.
    
    In order to derive the desired formula, we consider several subcases for $T\in\mathcal{T}_{\lambda,G}'$. 
    \begin{enumerate}[(a)]
    \item First, $T$ may have no $N$-steps, in which case, the rows of length $2$ are all filled with E-steps. We note that $v_1,v_2,v_3$ may not all be in the head since they are adjacent to all other vertices and thus may not be placed in two stable $2$-sets. Again, we break into subcases.
    \begin{enumerate}[(i)]
        \item Assume $v_1,v_2,v_3$ are all in the tail. Then we choose $C$ ordered stable $2$-sets from the $\beta$ stable $2$-sets in the graph and a non-increasing sequence in $G$ through the remaining vertices. This yields:
        \[
        \frac{\beta !}{(\beta-C)!}\cdot N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(3,2^{\beta-C})})
        \]
        positive SRH $G$-tabloids. We note that in the case where $D=1$, $\beta<C$ so the above term is set to equal zero. This is consistent with the impossibility of fitting three vertices in a tail of length $1$.
        \item Assume one vertex $v_j$ from the stable $3$-set is in the tail. We have three choices for which vertex is $v_j$. We then choose $C-1$ ordered stable $2$-sets out of the $\beta$ total stable $2$-sets. Then, we choose a position for the hook with the other two vertices from the $3$-set, for which we have $C$ possibilities. Finally, we choose a non-increasing sequence in $G$ through the remaining vertices. This yields:
        \[
        3\cdot \frac{\beta!}{(\beta-C+1)!}\cdot C \cdot N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{h-C+1},1)})
        \]
        positive $SRH$ $G$-tabloids. It is impossible for there to be exactly two of the three vertices $v_1,v_2,v_3$ in the tail since that would leave one of the vertices in the head with no other vertex with which to form a stable $2$-set.
    \end{enumerate}
        \item Second, $T$ may have a $NE$-hook starting from the top vertex in the tail. The $N$-step in this hook must be the only $N$-step in the diagram since the rest of the head is filled with E-hooks and the tail is a non-increasing sequence in $G$. This hook must be filled by the unique stable $3$-set $\{v_1,v_2,v_3\}$. We thus must choose $C-1$ ordered stable $2$-sets and a non-increasing sequence through the remaining vertices. Note that a non-increasing sequence followed by $v_1$ must still be a non-increasing sequence since $v_1$ is incomparable to all vertices besides $v_2$ and $v_3$. Hence these tabloids remain in $\mathcal{T}_{(2^{C}1^{D}), G}'$. This yields:
        \[
        \frac{\beta !}{(\beta-C+1)!}N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1})})
        \]
        negative SRH $G$-tabloids.
        \item Third, $T$ may have an EN-hook, made up of $v_1,v_2,v_3$, somewhere in the head. As before, the $N$-step in this hook must be the only $N$-step in the diagram since the rest of the head is filled with $E$-hooks and the tail forms a non-increasing sequence. There are $C-1$ positions for the EN-hook. We then choose $C-2$ ordered stable $2$-sets from the $\beta$ total stable $2$-sets to fill the other rows in the head. Moreover, there must be a hook of length $1$ to the northwest of the EN-hook to complete the diagram. There are $2(h-C+2)$ vertices left to choose from for this hook. Finally, we choose a non-increasing sequence through the remaining vertices. This yields:
        \[
        (C-1)\cdot \frac{\beta !}{(\beta-C+2)!}\cdot 2\cdot (\beta-C+2)\cdot N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1},1)})
        \]
        negative SRH $G$-tabloids.
    \end{enumerate}
    To derive the desired formula, we now compute the sum of all the above terms.
    \begin{align*}
        [s_{(2^C,1^D)}]X_{G}&=\frac{\beta !}{(\beta-C)!}\cdotN_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(3,2^{\beta-C})})+3\cdot \frac{\beta!}{(\beta-C+1)!}\cdot C \cdot N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1},1)})\\
        &-\frac{\beta !}{(\beta-C+1)!}N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1})})\\
        &-(C-1)\cdot \frac{\beta !}{(\beta-C+2)!}\cdot 2\cdot (\beta-C+2)\cdotN_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1},1)})\\
        &=\frac{\beta!}{(\beta-C+1)!}\big((\beta-C+1)\cdotN_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(3,2^{\beta-C})})+3C\cdotN_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1},1)})\\
        &-N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1})})-(2C-2)\cdotN_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1},1)}\big)\\
        &=\frac{\beta!}{(\beta-C+1)!}\big((\beta-C+1)\cdotN_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(3,2^{\beta-C})})+(2C+2)\cdotN_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1},1)})\\
        &-N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1})})\big)
    \end{align*}
    Since this formula has a negative term, it is necessary to prove it is always nonnegative in order to ensure Schur-positivity. To do so, we prove the inequality:
    \[
    N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1},1)})\ge N_{\mathrm{sp}}(K_{(2^{\beta-C+1})}).
    \]
    We have that $K_{(2^{\beta-C+1},1)}$ is the incomparability graph of the poset consisting of $\beta-C+1$ disjoint chains of length $2$ and $1$ disjoint chain of length $1$. We label the vertex in the chain of length $1$ by $u$.
    
    Consider any non-increasing sequence $v_1\nleq\cdots\nleq v_h$ through all the vertices of $K_{2^{\beta-C+1}}$. We can map any such path to the non-increasing sequence $v_1\nleq\cdots\nleq v_h\nleq u$ since $u$ is incomparable to all other vertices. Hence, this injection implies the desired inequality.
    
    
    \item Lastly, if $\lambda=1^{2\beta+3}$, by Proposition \ref{prop:inv_paths_tail}, the SRH $G$-tabloids are in bijection with the non-increasing sequences in $G$ so the desired formula follows.
    
    
\end{enumerate}
Since the independence number of $G$ is $3$ and there is one unique stable $3$-set, by Lemma \ref{lemma:independence_lemma}, the SRH $G$-tabloids we count may have at most one row of length $3$ (and no longer rows). Hence, cases 1, 2, 3, and 4 cover all possible partitions corresponding to nonzero coefficients.
    
    
    
\end{proof}

Together with Theorem \ref{thm:only_K32_may_b_s_pos}, Theorem \ref{thm:K32_s_pos} provides the following Schur-positivity classification for complete multipartite graphs.

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:classif}
A complete multipartite graph $K_{\lambda}$ for $\ell(\lambda)\ge 2$ is Schur-positive if and only if $\lambda_i\in\{1,2\}$ for $1\le i \le k$ or $\lambda=(3,2^{\beta})$ for $\beta\ge 1$.
\end{theorem}

Proposition \ref{prop:only_balanced_partitions} also classifies most complete multipartite graphs in terms of $\mathbf{e}$-positivity (since graphs which are not Schur-positive are not $\mathbf{e}$-positive). The question of whether $K_{(3,2^{\beta})}$ ($\beta\ge 1$) is $\mathbf{e}$-positive, however, remains open.
